By 2011, the "Cloud Race" had moved beyond web startups and into the heart of global infrastructure. For a telecom giant like Ericsson, the challenge was existential: could the massive, rigid hardware that powered the world’s mobile networks be transformed into flexible, software-defined entities? 

This period defined my journey from **Systems & Technology** to **Ericsson Research**, where we transitioned from asking "Can we virtualize a router?" to "How do we orchestrate a global cloud of them?"

---

## IPOS: Breaking the Hardware Shackles

When I joined the IPOS (Intellectual Property Operating System) team, the industry was at a crossroads. Traditional routers like the Smart Services Router (SSR) were hardware marvels, but they lacked the agility required for the emerging cloud market. My role as Product Owner was to lead the charge in making **IPOS available on virtual platforms**.

### Architecting the Virtual IP Platform (VIPP)
We built the **IPOS-REF**, a reference platform that allowed hundreds of engineers to develop features independent of physical hardware. This didn't just speed up development; it became the blueprint for the **Ericsson Virtual IP Platform (VIPP)**. 

To make virtual routers a reality, performance was non-negotiable. We achieved a **10x reduction in initialization time** and ported the forwarding plane to **DPDK APIs**, proving that software-based networking could finally approach carrier-grade speeds. This work, which earned "Most Valuable Contribution" awards in 2012 and 2013, gave Ericsson the footing needed to offer cloud-based routers to global operators.

## Ericsson Research: Mastering Cloud Complexity

In 2014, I moved to Ericsson Research to solve the "Day 2" problems of the cloud: orchestration, scale, and security. As networks became virtualized (NFV), they became exponentially more complex to manage.

### Simulating the Infinite: Matrix and ADL
How do you test a cloud orchestrator like **OpenStack** or **Ericsson Cloud Manager** without building a million-dollar data center? We built **Matrix**, a simulation engine that exposed RESTful APIs to simulate massive data center entities. 

I also worked on an **Application Description Language (ADL)**. By treating multi-tier cloud applications as Finite State Machines (FSMs), we could generate simulation code to observe application behavior at a massive scale. This work, presented at **HiPC 2014** and **ISEC 2015**, helped Ericsson understand the intricate dance of stateful messages between thousands of VMs—knowledge critical for building stable cloud orchestration policies.

### Securing the Virtual Frontier
Virtualization introduced new attack surfaces. I proposed and developed a lightweight **Early Warning System (EWS)** for detecting compromised or malfunctioning Network Function Virtualization (NFV) components. By enforcing traffic models directly in virtual switches, we created a way to catch anomalies before they could ripple through the network. This research was critical for ETSI-defined use cases and was presented at the **Network Security Conference** in Helsinki.

## Reflections on a Transformation
During these four years, I witnessed—and helped drive—the transformation of Ericsson from a hardware-first company to a cloud-native innovator. Whether it was leading the Bangalore site through **Agile transformation**, organizing **Hackathons** to harden our security, or scaling the **Converged Packet Gateway** to 1.1 million sessions, the goal remained the same: making the network as elastic as the cloud it serves.

The innovations we pioneered in virtual platforms and cloud simulation laid the groundwork for the 5G-ready, software-defined infrastructure that powers the world today.
